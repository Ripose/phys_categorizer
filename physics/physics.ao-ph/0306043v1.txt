Characteristics of Aerosol Spectral Optical Depths over Manora
Peak, Nainital − A High Altitude Station in the Central
Himalayas ⋆

Ram Sagar1, Brijesh Kumar1, U.C. Dumka1, K. Krishna Moorthy2 and P. Pant1

1 State Observatory, Manora Peak, Nainital, 263 129, Uttaranchal, India
2 Space Physics Laboratory, Vikram Sarabhai Space Centre, Thiruvananthapuram 695 022, India

Received xxxxx / Accepted xxxxx

Abstract. We present for the ﬁrst time spectral behaviour of aerosol optical depths (AODs) over Manora Peak,
Nainital located at an altitude of ∼ 2 km in the central Himalayas. The observations were carried out using a
Multi-Wavelength Solar Radiometer during January to December 2002. The primary features of the study are
(i) larger AOD during afternoon periods compared to forenoon, attributable to change in the ray path from
comparatively cleaner environment in the forenoon to polluted environment in the afternoon (ii) extremely low
AODs during local winter and a remarkable increase to high values in summer (iii) a distinct change in the spectral
dependencies of AODs from a relatively steeper spectra during winter to a shallower one in summer representing
both transparent (meteorological aerosols) and polluted summer (urban haze aerosols) skies.
During transparent days the AOD values at 0.38 µm lie usually below 0.1 while during dusty days, it lies between
0.1 to 0.9. Angstr¨om exponent for these two distributions are 0.97 ± 0.09 and 0.54 ± 0.07 respectively with
a mean value of 0.61 ± 0.06. The mean aerosol extinction law at Nainital during 2002 is best represented by
0.10λ−0.61, however during transparent days, which almost covers 40% of the time, the aerosol extinction law i.e.
0.02λ−0.97 is comparable to the Antarctic environment. A comparison of the total optical depths of the night-time
measurements taken during 1970’s with the day-time, underlines the fact that the summer sky pollution occurred
at those times also though less severely as of today.

Key words. Aerosol optical depth, Earth atmospheric extinction, water vapor, observatory site, Angstr¨om coeﬃ-
cients, High altitude aerosols

1. Introduction

Aerosols, both natural and anthropogenic, play an im-
portant role in atmospheric as well as astronomical sci-
ences. In the former, they aﬀect by imparting radiative
forcing and perturbing the radiative balance of the Earth-
atmosphere system as well as by degrading the envi-
ronment. To understand the eﬀects of aerosols on our
geo/biosphere system, it is essential to characterize their
physical, chemical and optical properties at as many lo-
cations as possible because of the regional nature of their
properties and the short lifetime (Moorthy et al. 1989,
1999; Satheesh et al. 2001). This will also help in building
up a comprehensive picture of global aerosol distribution
and also their potential environmental impacts. As most of
the aerosol sources are of terrestrial origin the variability

does help in evolving an average extinction law as well as

2. Experimental site, observational data and

analysis

The experimental site, Manora Peak,
just south of
Nainital, headquarters of the State Observatory, is located
in the Shivalik hills of central Himalayas (latitude = 29022′
N, longitude = 79027′ E) at an altitude of 1950 m (MSL).
The day-time observations at Manora Peak on aerosols
were taken for 163 days during January 2002 to December
2002 with a Multi-Wavelength Solar Radiometer (MWR)
designed and developed by SPL. The instrument provides
the columnar total optical depths at ten narrow band ﬁl-
ters (FWHM of 6 to 10 nm, at diﬀerent wavelengths) cen-
tered at 0.38, 0.40, 0.45, 0.50, 0.60, 0.65, 0.75, 0.85, 0.935
and 1.025 µm by making continuous spectral extinction
measurements of directly transmitted solar radiation. The
instrument works on the principle of ﬁlter wheel radiome-
ter (Shaw et al. 1973). More details of the instrument and
the principle of data reduction and error budget are de-
scribed in several earlier papers by Moorthy et al. (1997,
1999, 2001) and hence are not described here.

The observed ﬂux, F (λ, z), of the Sun at zenith dis-
tance, z, suﬀers extinction due to the Earth’s atmosphere.
It’s relation to the incident solar ﬂux, F0(λ), above the at-
mosphere, assuming the short time scale (hours) variation
of incident ﬂux as insigniﬁcant, is expressed by Lambert-
Beer law as

F (λ, z) = F0(λ)e−τλM(z)

where τλ is the columnar total optical depth (TOD) of
the Earth’s atmosphere at wavelength λ and M (z) is the
relative airmass at zenith distance, z. As the output Vλ
of the MWR at any wavelength is directly proportional
to F (λ, z), by solving a linear least squares ﬁt between
the natural logarithm of Vλ and the corresponding rela-
tive airmass, τλ is estimated as the slope of the regression
line (Moorthy et al. 1999), following the so called ’Langley
plot’ method. The relative airmass corresponding to each
observation is estimated following the general expression
given by Kondratyev (1969). A typical Langley plot ob-
tained for the forenoon data set of 28 December 2002 is

τozλ + τwλ), by ozone and water vapour contributes in

following formula gives an approximate determination of
the extinction only due to ozone assuming 0.232 atm-cm
ozone content for the observatory site at Nainital (Allen
1976).

The water vapour contributes very weakly at 0.5436
µm, 0.5744 µm and at few bands beyond 0.7 µm but sig-
niﬁcantly at 0.935 µm. In MWR, the wavelength 0.935 µm
is used to estimate columnar water vapour content follow-
ing Nair and Moorthy (1998) and this is used to correct
for water vapour absorption which is very weak at 0.75
µm and 0.85 µm bands (The mass absorption coeﬃcients
for water vapour at the aﬀected wavelengths 0.75, 0.85,
0.935 and 1.025 µm are 0.0001, 0.003, 32.5 and 0.0013 re-
spectively (Leckner 1978). Therefore, after correcting for
τRλ and τozλ, these bands will also carry τwλ apart from
τpλ). The optical depths due to water vapor and aerosols
are separated at these bands along with the simultaneous
determination of water vapour content, W (g/cm2), using
mass absorption coeﬃcients and an interpolation based
iterative scheme, which is described in detail elsewhere
(Moorthy et al. 1989; Nair and Moorthy 1998).

In addition to the above, there is a weak absorption by
NO2 at wavelengths below 0.45 µm. The typical value of
this is ∼ 0.006 (Tomasi et al. 1985). Subtracting τRλ and
ταλ from τλ the aerosol optical depths τpλ are estimated
for each data set of MWR observations.

2.3. Day-time MWR measurements

The MWR data obtained during the study period have
been analysed as described above and spectral AODs
are determined for each data sets. On certain days, the
Langley plots revealed occurrence of diﬀerent least square
ﬁt to the data with distinctly diﬀerent slopes for the
forenoon (FN) and afternoon (AN) parts of the same day
implying diﬀerent τλ. Therefore, the MWR data are anal-
ysed separately considering the FN and AN part of the
data as two independent sets. A consolidated log of the
data thus analysed is presented in Table 1. This shows
that we have analysed in all 241 data sets which were
obtained on 163 days of the year 2002. The data sets
are spread over all months of the year except for July
and August with maximum of 47 and minimum of 5 data
sets for the months of November and September 2002 (see
Table 1) respectively. The average number of data sets for
a month are thus about 24. The complete absence of data
in July and August and fewer observations in June and
September are due to the highly cloudy sky conditions
prevailing over the site, associated with the Indian sum-
mer monsoon (Asnani 1993). The τpλ for each observation
day are given in Table 2, with the forenoon and afternoon
data sets tabulated separately. Filters at wavelengths 0.40
and 0.60 µm could be installed only on 11 October 2002
and 10 December 2002 respectively. The number of data
sets at these wavelengths are therefore less. However, they
will not aﬀect the conclusions drawn in this paper. During
the observations, airmass ranges < 4, 4 to 8 and > 8 cor-
respond to 9.5%, 36.5% and 54% of the data sets. The
determination of τλ can therefore be considered quite re-
liable as in more than 90% cases, the dense temporal data
points have spanned over a large airmass range.

3. Results and discussions

3.1. Temporal variation of AODs

Fig. 2. shows the temporal variations of water vapour con-
tent and aerosol optical depths at 8 wavelengths excluding
0.6 µm and 0.935 µm. The 0.6 µm wavelength was intro-
duced only in December 2002 and the wavelength 0.935
µm was used to estimate integrated water vapour content
and to correct for water vapour absorption which is very
weak at 0.75 µm and 0.85 µm. However, in performing
these corrections, it is noticed that for four observed data
sets the τpλ values at 0.85 µm, 0.935 µm and 1.025 µm
are such that the water vapour content could not be de-
termined. Since MWR measurements at 0.935 µm were
used to estimate water vapor content, we have not con-
sidered τpλ at 0.935 µm for any further analysis of aerosol
properties. At 0.4 µm also the data is some what limited
due to its late-introduction, nevertheless it is included and
gives the variations from October 2002 onwards. During
15 June to 15 September no observations could be taken
due to either cloudy or rainy sky conditions. The data
cover the range from excellent ”coronal” days where τpλ
is very low (< 0.1) to ”absorbent” (with high τpλ > 0.5
) days where summer dust from the adjoining plain areas
is present in the skies above the site. Long term temporal
variations and other results are described below.

the same period the mean arrival wind direction gradually
shifts from Southerly to Westerly. For the location of the
Observatory this would mean a shift in the airmass from
the Southern Indian land mass in winter to the Western
land mass during summer, when the winds arrive from
the vast arid regions of North-West India and regions ly-
ing further to its West. This change in the airmass type,
obviously is mostly responsible for the rapid build-up in
the AOD over the station after March as the arid airmass
is known to transport large amounts of desert/mineral
aerosols from the West Asian and Indian deserts, lying
to the West/South-West of this station. The role of wind
blown dust from the deserts in increasing turbidity over
Northern Indian regions have been suggested by Mani et
al. (1969). Recently, based on satellite data analysis, Li
and Ramanathan (2002) have shown the Eastward trans-
port of West-Asian deserts aerosols across the Northern
Arabian sea towards the West coast of India. Thus the ob-
served sharp increase in the AOD from middle of March is
mostly attributed to this. In addition to the advection by
airmass, the increased solar heating of the land mass over
the lower plains adjacent to the site during the summer
season would results in increased convective mixing and

The water vapour content, W, is plotted in the top
panels of Figs. 2 and 3 to study its possible eﬀect on
aerosols. The W is below 0.2 g/cm2 for 80% of the mea-
surements with a mean value of 0.1 g/cm2, while it is
greater than 0.2 g/cm2 mostly in summer and in the
month of October. An increase in W (when higher than
3 g/cm2 ) is known to cause a non-linear increase in τp
at all wavelengths covered by MWR (Nair and Moorthy
1998; Moorthy et al. 2001). As the values of W are very
low at the site, the temporal variations in W and AOD are
quite similar (see Figs. 2 and 3), with the only exception
for the month of October, where AODs are small despite
large (> 0.2 g/cm2) values of W. This indicates that the
variations in AOD and W are brought about mostly by
the same processes related to local and synoptic changes
in the meteorological conditions.

b) Intra-day variations in AODs

On several days the MWR data showed that the
Langley plots can have two slopes in a day; one for the
forenoon and the other, distinctively diﬀerent, for the af-
ternoon. In all, there were 78 days when both FN and AN
AODs could be estimated separately (see Table 5). For all
these cases the deviation ∆ = [τpλ,AN − τpλ,F N ] was esti-
mated. The mean values of ∆ at 0.38 µm are 0.09 ± 0.03,
0.15 ± 0.14, 0.25 ± 0.13, 0.16 ± 0.12 and 0.26 ± 0.23 during
January-February, March, April-June, October-November
and December 2002 respectively. However, this variation
decreases with increasing wavelengths and is less appar-
ent at 0.75 µm. Errors in measurements indicate an un-
certainty of ±0.04 in the ∆ values. A comparison of this
error with the ∆ values listed in Table 5 indicates that :-
(i) there are only three days during the year 2002,
20/04, 23/04 and 14/06 where the negative values of ∆

3.2. Spectral variation of AODs

The wavelength variation of AOD, τpλ, is expressed in
simple case by the following power law (Angstr¨om 1961),

τpλ = βλ−α

(7)

where the parameters β and α vary widely and depend on
the content (loading) and size distribution of aerosols re-
spectively. The wavelength exponent α is a measure of the
relative dominance of ﬁne, sub-micrometer sized aerosols
over the coarse aerosols while β is a measure of the to-
tal aerosol load. Higher value of α signiﬁes increased rel-
ative abundance of ﬁne particles. The observations indi-
cate that α lies between 0.5 and 1.6 for the meteorological
aerosols in absence of any man made pollutants such as
urban haze or volcanic aerosols (Burki et al. 1995). The
mean α estimated under diﬀerent oceanic conditions also
lie in this range (Satheesh et al. 2001). The lower limit is
approached when the relative abundance of larger parti-
cles is high for example, a value of α = 0.71 was obtained
during Pinatubo volcanic erruptions (Schuster and Parrao
2001), and the upper limit witnesses the minimum pollu-
tion of the sky for example α = 1.67 is approached on
clearest sky with smallest aerosol particles at San Pedro
Martir (Schuster and Parrao 2001), α = 2.6 at CTIO,
Chile (Guti´errez-Moreno et al. 1982) and a mean value
of 2.04 was obtained for the Nainital site during 1970’s
(Kumar et al. 2000).

4. Conclusions

The main conclusions of our study are the following.

variations at short and long time scales.

2. There is a remarkable increase in spectral AOD from
their very low values in winter to high values in sum-
mer. During winter season, the AODs are of magni-
tude comparable to the Antarctic environment, while
during summer they are of the order seen in typical
continental regions.

3. In the short time scale, the forenoon AOD values are
lower than the corresponding values in the afternoon,
attributable to change in the ray path from compara-
tively cleaner environment in the forenoon to polluted
environment in the afternoon.

4. The aerosol size distributions observed to be bimodal
representing transparent (meteorological aerosols) and
polluted summer (urban haze aerosols) skies. During
transparent days the aerosol optical depth at 0.38µm
lie usually below 0.1 while during dusty days, it lies
between 0.1 to 0.9. The mean aerosol extinction law at
the site during 2002 is best represented by 0.10λ−0.61.
5. During transparent days, for 40% of the observable
days, the aerosol extinction law i.e. 0.02λ−0.97 is com-
parable to the Antarctic environment.

6. A comparison of the total optical depths of the night-
time measurements, taken during 1970’s, with the day-
time underlines the fact that the summer sky pollution
occurred at those times also, though less severely as of
today.

7. The water vapour content, W, lies < 0.2 g/cm2, for

80% of the clear days.
